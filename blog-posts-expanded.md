---

I just ran through 13 retailer inventory reports for Q4 2025. Not summaries. Not headlines. The actual filings.

Here is what the data says — and more importantly, what it means for your buying posture right now.

**The Short Version**

This is not a distress cycle. Big-box general merchandise is controlled. The real pressure is building in apparel, footwear, and Amazon FBA. If you are still treating every category the same, you are either overpaying somewhere or leaving money on the table somewhere else.

**General Merchandise: Controlled, Not Flooding**

Walmart is up 4% YoY. Target is down 3.4%. Kohl's is down 6.8%. Dillard's is essentially flat at plus 2%.

That is not the profile of a broad-based distress cycle. Walmart managing inventory at plus 4% after years of overcorrection is a sign of discipline, not weakness. Target and Kohl's actively reducing inventory means they are protecting margin — not dumping. When sellers have pricing discipline, buyers need to have bidding discipline. Do not let the volume fool you into thinking reserve prices are going to collapse in general merchandise.

My recommendation for GM: stay disciplined. Bid on value, not on the assumption that sellers are desperate. They are not.

**Apparel and Footwear: This Is Where the Real Opportunity Is**

This is where the numbers get interesting.

American Eagle is up 10% YoY. Gap is up 7%. Adidas is up nearly 17%. When branded apparel inventories move into high single to mid-teens territory, here is what happens in sequence: sellers protect their primary retail channels first. The overflow — the inventory that does not fit the primary story — gets pushed to the secondary market. And it comes with looser reserve pricing, more inconsistent lot quality, and broader category mix.

That is not a bad thing if you know how to work it. Larger lot sizes with wider quality dispersion create opportunity for buyers who can grade and sort efficiently. The buyers who get burned are the ones pricing off MSRP on lots with heavy markdown and damaged mix. The buyers who win are the ones using real resale comps by channel.

My recommendation for apparel and footwear: press harder. The supply is real, the reserve pressure is real, and the buyers who move fast with accurate pricing models will capture the best loads.

**Home Improvement: Selective, Not Broken**

Home Depot is up 10.1% YoY. Lowe's is flat at minus 0.6%.

The bifurcation here matters. Home Depot's build raises the probability of more secondary market softness in discretionary home, seasonal, and décor — the categories that move with project spending and tend to have longer turns in liquidation. Lowe's flat performance does not confirm a broad home improvement glut.

Know the difference between a Home Depot-sourced category and a Lowe's-sourced one before you bid. Spring demand in tools and hardware will pull some of this inventory faster than it looks on paper. But discretionary home décor and seasonal? Price carefully and model your turns conservatively.

**The Amazon Signal Everyone Should Be Watching**

Amazon came in at $38.3 billion in inventory, up 12.0% year over year. That is a large number in absolute terms, and it deserves a separate conversation.

When Amazon's inventory builds, the downstream effect on the secondary market is not immediate — it depends on what Amazon does with FBA storage fee pressure. When Amazon tightens storage fees to clear out fulfillment centers, FBA sellers with slow-moving SKUs get squeezed. That inventory moves into Amazon's own Liquidation Auctions program or gets pushed into other secondary channels.

At plus 12%, the question is not whether Amazon's inventory is high. The question is whether Amazon tightens FBA storage fee pressure in Q1 and Q2 2026 to push sellers to move slow inventory.

If they do, you will see more FBA liquidation lots hit the secondary market — typically mixed condition, broad category, inconsistent lot quality. That is good for volume buyers who know how to sort. It is tough for buyers who need predictable condition grades or who flip for a living.

The leading indicator to watch is not the inventory number itself. It is Amazon's Q1 2026 FBA fee announcements and storage surcharge communications. That is what tells you whether the 12% build becomes secondary market supply — and when.

**Bottom Line Buying Posture for Early 2026**

Press harder in apparel and footwear. Real supply, real reserve pressure, real opportunity for buyers with accurate pricing models.

Stay disciplined in big box general merchandise. Sellers have pricing control. Bid on value, not on desperation that is not there.

Be selective in home improvement. Spring demand is a real tailwind in tools and hardware. Discretionary home and seasonal need conservative turn modeling.

Watch Amazon closely. The inventory build is real. Whether it becomes secondary market supply depends on storage fee decisions in the next 60 days.

# BLOG POST B
title: "Reverse Logistics Is Now a Trillion-Dollar Supply Chain. Most Companies Are Still Running It Like It's 2005."

date: 2026-03-15

meta_description: "The reverse logistics market is on track to hit $1.75 trillion by 2035. Curtis Greve explains what that means for retailers, manufacturers, and operators who are still treating returns as a back-office problem."
---

The numbers are no longer small enough to ignore.

The reverse logistics market — returns processing, excess inventory disposition, secondary market redistribution, refurbishment, recycling — came in at an estimated $872.6 billion in 2025. The 2026 forecast is $936 billion. By 2035, the projection is $1.75 trillion, compounding at 7.3% annually.

That is not a niche. That is a major global industry. And most of the companies operating inside it are still running it like a back-office problem.

**What "Trillion-Dollar Supply Chain" Actually Means for Operators**

Let me translate the market size number into what it means at the company level, because market projections are only useful if you know how to act on them.

First: reverse logistics is no longer the shadow of your forward supply chain. It is its own industry, with its own economics, its own buyer networks, its own technology infrastructure, and its own performance benchmarks. The companies that built strategy around that reality five years ago are pulling ahead. The ones still treating returns as a cost to minimize are falling behind — and the gap is widening as volume grows.

Second: recovery performance is becoming a competitive separator. The difference between a disciplined liquidation and disposition strategy and an unmanaged one is not a rounding error. For a retailer processing $100 million in annual returns, the gap between average recovery and best-in-class recovery is often $15 million to $25 million per year. That is not theoretical. That is the range I see consistently in program assessments.

Third: as the market approaches $1.75 trillion, the ad-hoc practices that worked at smaller scale will stop working. The buyers who have historically absorbed liquidation inventory through informal relationships and handshake deals are being replaced — or supplemented — by structured buyer networks, data-driven pricing, and platforms that bring transparency to what has always been an opaque market. Retailers who are still selecting liquidation partners based on who called last will find themselves outcompeted by the ones who are running structured programs with performance metrics, competitive bidding, and clear grading standards.

Fourth: speed matters as much as price. The companies that shorten their reverse cycle time — from the moment a return hits a DC door to the moment it is sold and removed — capture meaningfully more value. Every week of holding time is a week of depreciation, space cost, and handling cost. The best operators in this industry move product in days, not weeks. That operational discipline is worth more than chasing incremental price on a slow-moving program.

**Why Most Companies Are Still Behind**

I have been in this industry for over 40 years. I have run reverse logistics at Walmart, GENCO, and Inmar. I have seen the inside of hundreds of programs. And the single most consistent finding is this: leadership with no reverse logistics background is running reverse logistics.

Companies dump the returns function on whoever has bandwidth. The new executive over reverse logistics often has zero background in liquidation, vendor management, or secondary market pricing. Nobody in the room knows what good looks like. And because nobody knows what good looks like, mediocre deals feel fine. Vendors get long-term contracts they did not earn. Recovery rates that would embarrass a best-in-class operator get reported as acceptable.

That gap is expensive. And it is fixable.

**What to Do About It**

If your company is in the $500 million to $3 billion revenue range and you have not had your reverse logistics and liquidation program independently assessed in the last three years, you almost certainly have significant dollars leaving the table every quarter. The market has changed. Buyer networks have changed. Technology has changed. What your program was built for in 2020 is not optimized for 2026.

I work with retailers, manufacturers, and 3PLs to benchmark current program performance, identify the specific gaps, and build a roadmap to close them. If you want to understand what that looks like for your operation, reach out directly at curtis@cgreve.com or through the contact form at cgreve.com.

The market is going to $1.75 trillion. The question is how much of your share you are going to capture versus give away.

What is the biggest problem major retailers have in managing their returns?

It is not software. It is not vendor selection. It is not grading standards or channel mix or reserve pricing — though all of those matter.

The biggest problem is leadership with no reverse logistics experience.

**What This Looks Like in Practice**

The pattern is consistent across companies of every size. A retailer reorganizes. The reverse logistics function gets consolidated under a supply chain VP who came up through procurement or transportation. Or it gets dropped into operations under someone who runs forward distribution. Or — and this happens more than it should — it gets handed to whoever had the bandwidth when the last person left.

The new executive is smart. They work hard. They ask the right questions in vendor meetings. But they have never negotiated a liquidation contract, never built a grading program from scratch, never managed a buyer network, never run an RFP for a third-party liquidation provider. They do not know what best-in-class recovery looks like. They do not know what the levers are. They do not know which vendor claims are reasonable and which ones are not.

So they get taken advantage of. Not maliciously. Just naturally. Vendors and liquidators optimize for their own economics. If the person across the table does not know the benchmarks, the deal reflects that.

**What It Costs**

I have watched Fortune 100 companies agree to terrible liquidation contracts and feel good about it. Not because they were careless. Because nobody in the room knew what good looked like.

The math on this is straightforward. A retailer processing $100 million in returns annually at 30 cents on the dollar recovery is leaving roughly $15 million to $25 million per year on the table compared to a best-in-class program running at 55 to 65 cents recovery. That delta does not show up as a line item on the P&L. It just shows up as a number that nobody questions because nobody knows it should be higher.

I have seen companies where a single contract renegotiation — after a proper program assessment — added more than $10 million in annual recovery. One contract. One assessment. One conversation with someone who knew what the number should be.

That is not an edge case. That is what happens when you bring real expertise into a function that has been running on institutional inertia.

**Why This Keeps Happening**

Reverse logistics is not a high-profile function. It does not get the executive attention that forward distribution, transportation, or procurement does. When things are going adequately — not great, just adequately — nobody asks hard questions.

The other issue is that there are not many people in the world with deep, operating-level expertise in both the seller side and the buyer side of the secondary market. Most consultants in this space have either run a liquidation company or worked on the retail side. Very few have done both at scale.

I spent five years running Walmart's reverse logistics division, 14 years as President of GENCO managing return center and liquidation operations for Target, Best Buy, Walmart, and others, and a decade at Inmar building the largest consumer goods liquidation service in North America. title: "March 2026 Liquidation Market Brief: Tariffs, Bigger Lots, and Where to Bid With Confidence"
slug: march-2026-liquidation-market-brief

---

Three things are reshaping the wholesale liquidation landscape right now, and smart buyers and sellers need to be positioned ahead of each one.

**The Tariff Ruling: Real Money, But It Will Not Come Fast**

A federal judge recently ordered U.S. Customs and Border Protection to refund IEEPA tariffs and stop collecting them on imports still in the liquidation process. For liquidators and secondary market buyers, this matters more than the headline suggests.

Here is why. Retailers who front-loaded inventory in late 2025 to beat tariff implementation are now sitting on excess stock they over-purchased. They bought ahead of demand to protect against cost increases. Now that the tariff situation is shifting, the urgency to hold that inventory is lower — and the pressure to move it is real.

The result: expect more forced selling in Q2 2026. Bigger lots. More motivated sellers. More negotiating leverage for buyers who are positioned and capitalized to move fast.

The opportunity is coming. The timeline is not instantaneous. Do not expect it to hit all at once in April. This is a Q2 story that builds across the quarter as retailers assess their positions and make disposition decisions. Be ready. Have capital available. Know your channel capacity before the volume arrives.

**Lot Sizes Are Growing. Volatility Is Increasing. Discipline Wins.**

This is not new for experienced buyers, but it is worth stating clearly: the days of relatively uniform lot quality and predictable pricing are increasingly behind us.

Supply is rising across the board. Lot sizes are getting bigger — B-Stock's recent recommerce data confirms this trend. And when lot sizes grow and quality dispersion widens, pricing becomes more volatile, not less. You will see great lots and terrible lots in the same category in the same week. Reserve pricing will be inconsistent. Condition grade claims will vary.

There is one response to this environment: discipline. Specifically, pricing discipline.

If you are still pricing loads off MSRP, you are going to get burned in this market. MSRP-based pricing has always been a lazy shortcut, but in a high-volatility environment it becomes a direct path to losses. The only pricing that matters right now is current verified retail pricing — what the item actually sells for in the relevant resale channel today — combined with a real condition mix assumption and your full cost stack.

Buyers who have that model built will outperform. Buyers who are guessing will have a difficult Q2.

**DIY and Home Improvement: Bid With Confidence**

Not everything is a cautionary story. Spring demand is pulling tool, hardware, and home improvement categories ahead of the pack right now, and the data supports it.

Harvard's Joint Center for Housing Studies remodeling data confirms continued early 2026 growth in home improvement spending. Home Depot's inventory build — up over 10% YoY — combined with spring project demand means faster turns and stronger recovery in DIY-adjacent categories compared to the rest of the secondary market.

If you are buying returns or overstocks in tools, hardware, garden, and home improvement right now, bid with more confidence than you would in a flat market. The turns are faster. Recovery is stronger. And spring demand gives you a natural window to move inventory efficiently.

This is not a permanent condition — summer will tell a different story. But for Q2 2026 buying, home improvement is one of the clearest bright spots in the liquidation market.

**How to Position Right Now**

Be ready for Q2 forced selling out of the tariff inventory overhang. Have capital and channel capacity available before the volume arrives.

Price everything off current verified retail, not MSRP. Build your condition mix assumptions from real data, not optimistic grades.

Lean into DIY and home improvement while spring demand supports faster turns and better recovery.

Stay disciplined in broadline GM. The supply is real, but so is the quality dispersion. Know what you can process and price accordingly.

# BLOG POST E
title: "How I Would Play the Wholesale Liquidation Market Right Now: A March 2026 Buyer's Playbook"
slug: wholesale-liquidation-buying-playbook-march-2026
date: 2026-03-05
category: Liquidation Buying
meta_description: "Curtis Greve breaks down exactly how an experienced liquidation buyer should be positioned in March 2026 — where to bid tighter, where to be aggressive, and what signals are driving the market."
---

The liquidation market in March 2026 is not a uniform environment. Two competing forces are operating simultaneously: a broad post-January inventory correction pushing more supply into secondary channels, and a seasonal DIY demand surge pulling specific categories in the opposite direction.

If you are treating every load the same right now, you are almost certainly leaving money on the table somewhere — or overpaying somewhere else.

Here is how I would play it.

**Broadline Mixed Returns: Bid Tighter**

On Walmart-style and Amazon-style general merchandise mixed returns, my posture right now is tighter pricing, not looser. Here is why.

Capacity is being built because the pressure is real. DHL recently launched and expanded a multi-client returns network designed to streamline returns processing at scale. That is not a response to a temporary blip — it is infrastructure investment to handle a sustained increase in returns volume. More processing capacity means more lots hitting the market.

B-Stock's recommerce data confirms the same trend from the buyer side: lot sizes are increasing and category recovery is shifting. In a high-supply, larger-lot environment, quality dispersion widens. You will see strong lots and weak lots in the same category in the same week. Reserve pricing becomes less predictable. The buyers who win are the ones with tight, accurate pricing models. The ones who lose are the ones who anchor on what a category returned six months ago.

The practical advice: on broadline GM mixed returns, bid based on current verified retail pricing and a conservative condition mix assumption. Expect more supply competition in Q2 from tariff-related forced selling. Do not assume today's pricing holds through the summer.

**DIY-Adjacent Categories: Be Aggressive — But Selectively**

Here is where the opportunity is right now. Spring demand for tools, hardware, garden, and home improvement categories is a real market force, and it is running counter to the broader post-January inventory correction.

The data supports it. Harvard's remodeling research confirms continued early 2026 growth in home improvement spending. Home Depot's inventory build at plus 10% YoY combined with active spring project demand means faster inventory turns and stronger recovery in these categories compared to the rest of the liquidation market.

If you are buying returns and overstocks in DIY-adjacent categories right now, bid with more confidence than you would in flat conditions. The turns are faster. Recovery is stronger. And you have a natural window — roughly through late Q2 — before summer conditions shift the picture.

The selective qualifier matters. I am talking about tools, hardware, garden, seasonal home improvement — not all home categories. Discretionary home décor, furniture, and fashion-adjacent home goods do not have the same demand tailwind. Price those conservatively.

**Make Speed Your Structural Advantage**

In a market where supply is rising and lots are getting bigger, speed is a competitive weapon that does not get enough attention.

Fast pay and fast pull gets you first look when sellers are clearing congestion. Sellers dealing with large volumes and limited warehouse space are not just optimizing for price — they are optimizing for certainty, speed, and logistics simplicity. A buyer who can close fast, pay clean, and pull quickly has leverage that shows up as better deal flow over time, not just on individual transactions.

If you are consistently the fast buyer in your seller relationships, you will see loads before slower buyers do. In a rising-supply environment, that first-look advantage compounds.

**The Pricing Discipline Point**

I will say this one more time because it costs buyers real money when they get it wrong: if you are pricing off MSRP, you will overpay in this market.

MSRP has always been a starting point, not a pricing model. In a high-volatility environment with wider quality dispersion, it is actively dangerous. The only pricing that matters is current verified retail — what the item actually sells for in the channel you are selling into, today, with the condition mix you actually receive.

Build that model. Verify it regularly. Do not let a strong load history in a category make you complacent about what the current market looks like.

**Putting It Together**

Bid tighter on broadline GM. Expect more supply and wider quality dispersion through Q2.

Bid more aggressively on DIY and home improvement while spring demand supports faster turns and better recovery.

Use speed as leverage. Fast pay and fast pull earns you first-look relationships that pay out over time.

Price everything off current verified retail. Not MSRP. Not what worked six months ago.
