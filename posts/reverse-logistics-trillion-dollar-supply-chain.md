The numbers are no longer small enough to ignore.

The reverse logistics market — returns processing, excess inventory disposition, secondary market redistribution, refurbishment, recycling — came in at an estimated $872.6 billion in 2025. The 2026 forecast is $936 billion. By 2035, the projection is $1.75 trillion, compounding at 7.3% annually.

That is not a niche. That is a major global industry. And most of the companies operating inside it are still running it like a back-office problem.

## What "Trillion-Dollar Supply Chain" Actually Means for Operators

Let me translate the market size number into what it means at the company level, because market projections are only useful if you know how to act on them.

**First: reverse logistics is no longer the shadow of your forward supply chain.** It is its own industry, with its own economics, its own buyer networks, its own technology infrastructure, and its own performance benchmarks. The companies that built strategy around that reality five years ago are pulling ahead. The ones still treating returns as a cost to minimize are falling behind — and the gap is widening as volume grows.

**Second: recovery performance is becoming a competitive separator.** The difference between a disciplined liquidation and disposition strategy and an unmanaged one is not a rounding error. For a retailer processing $100 million in annual returns, the gap between average recovery and best-in-class recovery is often $15 million to $25 million per year. That is not theoretical. That is the range I see consistently in program assessments.

**Third: as the market approaches $1.75 trillion, the ad-hoc practices that worked at smaller scale will stop working.** The buyers who have historically absorbed liquidation inventory through informal relationships and handshake deals are being replaced — or supplemented — by structured buyer networks, data-driven pricing, and platforms that bring transparency to what has always been an opaque market. Retailers who are still selecting liquidation partners based on who called last will find themselves outcompeted by the ones who are running structured programs with performance metrics, competitive bidding, and clear grading standards.

**Fourth: speed matters as much as price.** The companies that shorten their reverse cycle time — from the moment a return hits a DC door to the moment it is sold and removed — capture meaningfully more value. Every week of holding time is a week of depreciation, space cost, and handling cost. The best operators in this industry move product in days, not weeks. That operational discipline is worth more than chasing incremental price on a slow-moving program.

## Why Most Companies Are Still Behind

I have been in this industry for over 40 years. I have run reverse logistics at Walmart, GENCO, and Inmar. I have seen the inside of hundreds of programs. And the single most consistent finding is this: leadership with no reverse logistics background is running reverse logistics.

Companies dump the returns function on whoever has bandwidth. The new executive over reverse logistics often has zero background in liquidation, vendor management, or secondary market pricing. Nobody in the room knows what good looks like. And because nobody knows what good looks like, mediocre deals feel fine. Vendors get long-term contracts they did not earn. Recovery rates that would embarrass a best-in-class operator get reported as acceptable.

That gap is expensive. And it is fixable.
